1. Expanded goals for Phase 2 – Consonant Bootcamp
Phase name: Consonant Bootcamp (A0)
By the end of this phase, a true beginner should be able to:
Recognise all basic 14 consonant letters: ㄱ ㄴ ㄷ ㄹ ㅁ ㅂ ㅅ ㅇ ㅈ ㅊ ㅋ ㅌ ㅍ ㅎ.[5]
Understand the idea that Hangeul consonants are stylised diagrams of mouth/tongue positions (lips, tongue, throat).[6][7][8]
Know the dual role of ㅇ: silent at the start, “ng” at the end of a block.[7][1]
Have an intuitive feel for plain vs aspirated vs tense series (e.g., ㄱ / ㅋ / ㄲ, ㄷ / ㅌ / ㄸ, ㅂ / ㅍ / ㅃ, ㅅ / ㅆ, ㅈ / ㅊ / ㅉ).[4][9]
Read and build a small set of CV syllables using consonants + vowels from Phase 1 (e.g., 가, 거, 고, 구, 나, 다, 마, 바).[9][7]

2. New concept screens (more theory, small bites)
You currently have a single high‑density description: “Learn Korean consonants, including plain, aspirated, and tense sounds…”. Below is a split into 5 focused concept screens.[1]
Screen C1 – What do consonants show?
Key ideas:
Hangeul consonants were designed to show how your speech organs move: tongue, lips, and throat.[8][6][7]
For example:
ㄴ looks like the tongue touching the top of the mouth.
ㅁ looks like the outline of lips.
ㅇ relates to the throat / empty mouth.[5][8]
Micro‑questions (no answers in the stem):
When you say “n” in English, which part touches the top of your mouth?
Which body part moves most for ㅁ (m‑sound):
A) Lips
B) Tongue
C) Throat
Only after the learner chooses do you show “Correct/Wrong” feedback + explanation.

Screen C2 – Meet the core consonants (shapes + basic sounds)
Group consonants by articulation and simplicity:
Lips group: ㅁ (m), ㅂ (b/p), ㅍ (pʰ).
Tongue front: ㄴ (n), ㄷ (d/t), ㅌ (tʰ), ㄹ (r/l).
Back of tongue / velar: ㄱ (g/k), ㅋ (kʰ).
Fricatives & others: ㅅ (s), ㅎ (h), ㅇ (silent/ng), ㅈ (j), ㅊ (ch).[3][7][9]
For each, show:
Big glyph.
One simple English hint (approximate, not perfect).
Audio button with single‑consonant sound (as close as possible) and an example syllable (e.g., 가, 나, 마).
Micro‑questions examples:
“Which consonant uses both lips together:
ㄴ, ㅁ, or ㄱ?”
“If you say ‘g/k’ at the back of your mouth, which consonant group is this?”

Screen C3 – Plain vs aspirated vs tense (concept)
Content:
Use one set (ㄱ / ㅋ / ㄲ) to illustrate:
Plain (lenis): ㄱ – regular, soft “g/k”, little air.
Aspirated: ㅋ – more air puff out (you can feel it on your hand in front of your mouth).
Tense: ㄲ – strongly pressed, stiff throat and tongue, less obvious air release.[3][4][9]
Explain that Korean has series:
ㄱ / ㅋ / ㄲ
ㄷ / ㅌ / ㄸ
ㅂ / ㅍ / ㅃ
ㅈ / ㅊ / ㅉ
ㅅ / ㅆ
You do not need perfect production now—just awareness.
Micro‑questions:
When you say the aspirated sound, which has more air on your hand:
ㄱ or ㅋ?
Which type feels “tighter” in your throat: plain or tense?

Screen C4 – Special letter ㅇ
Content (mostly you already have):
At the start of a block, ㅇ is silent, just a placeholder for vowel‑starting syllables (아, 어 etc.).[7][1]
At the end of a block (받침 position), ㅇ is pronounced like “ng” in English “king”.[1][7]
Micro‑questions:
In “아”, is the ㅇ sound silent or like “ng”?
In “방”, what sound does the final consonant make:
A) “m”
B) “ng”
C) silent
Again, options only; no answer printed.

Screen C5 – Consonants inside syllable blocks
Connect Phase 1 + Phase 2:
A Korean syllable can be:
C + V (e.g., 가, 너, 모),
C + V + C (e.g., 감, 밥) – CVC later.
Consonant always sits at the top‑left or top of the block; vowels attach to the right/below.[2][10][7]
Micro‑questions:
Which part of the block is the consonant in 가?
Is 나 a CV or CVC syllable?

3. Activity 1 – Visual consonant recognition
Your Phase 2 TSX already has “visualQuestions” and “visualCorrect”. Here’s richer content + rules.[1]
Activity 1A – Shape‑to‑sound gallery
Learner scrolls through consonants; for each:
Large glyph (e.g., ㄴ).
Mouth diagram (icon) or 1–2 words: “tongue touches top front”.
Audio: consonant + example syllable (“ㄴ… 나”).
Short self‑check prompts:
“Try saying ‘n’ like in ‘no’. Feel where your tongue is—front/top.”
Micro‑questions:
“This consonant is made with the tongue at the front. Which is it?” (options show ㄴ, ㅁ, ㅇ etc.)
“This consonant’s shape looks like lips. Which one: ㄴ, ㅁ, or ㄱ?”
Activity 1B – Visual multiple‑choice (no answer in stem)
Each item:
Question text never includes the correct consonant glyph. For example:
“Choose the consonant that uses both lips (m‑sound).”
“Choose the consonant that usually sounds like ‘n’ at the start of a syllable.”
Display 3–4 consonant glyph options.
Learner taps one.
Feedback rules (apply everywhere in Phase 2):
If correct:
Play “Correct” sound tone.
Highlight the chosen option in green.
Show short explanation: “ㅁ uses both lips—it’s similar to m‑sound.”
If incorrect:
Play “Wrong” sound tone.
Mark the chosen option with a red border.
Also highlight the correct option in green.
Show short explanation: “ㅁ is lips (m). You selected ㄴ, which is tongue‑front.”
The TSX pattern visualCorrect ? "맞아요! Correct!" : "Oops! Incorrect." can be replaced with this state‑driven styling + explanation.[1]

4. Activity 2 – Ear training for consonants
Your file already has listeningQuestions and seriesQuestions for plain/aspirated/tense series. Enrich them with more granular items.[1]
Activity 2A – Identify consonant in a simple CV syllable
Per item:
Play a syllable using a simple vowel from Phase 1 (e.g., 가, 나, 마, 바, 사, 자).[4][9]
Display 3–4 consonant glyphs as options: ㄱ, ㄴ, ㅁ, ㅂ etc.
Prompt: “Which consonant do you hear at the start of this syllable?”
Feedback uses the same red/green + sounds pattern, with explanation:
“You heard [가]. The initial sound is similar to ‘g/k’, so the consonant is ㄱ.”
Activity 2B – Plain vs aspirated vs tense contrast
Per series (e.g., ㄱ/ㅋ/ㄲ):
Show three buttons labelled “A”, “B”, “C” (or the glyphs themselves), but the stem does not contain the correct one by name.
Play one sound or a syllable pair like 가 vs 카 vs 까.
Prompt examples:
“Which version has the strongest puff of air?”
“Which version sounds ‘strongest’ / more tense?”
Learner chooses; explanation after:
“This was ㅋ (aspirated). You can feel more air on your hand.”
“This was ㄲ (tense). The voice started tighter and shorter.”
Micro‑questions for metacognition:
“Which series do you find hardest:
g/k series,
d/t series,
b/p series?”
Store this to tailor later practice.

5. Activity 3 – Build and read CV syllables
This corresponds to syllableQuestions / syllableCorrect in the file.[1]
Activity 3A – Build syllable from consonant + vowel
Per item:
Show a consonant and a vowel separately (e.g., ㄱ + ㅏ).
Prompt: “Tap the correct syllable block.”
Show 3–4 options: 가 / 개 / 고 / 구 etc.
Feedback: red/green + “Correct/Wrong” tones, with explanation:
“ㄱ + ㅏ builds 가. You chose 개, which uses ㅐ.”
Activity 3B – Read syllables out loud (self‑evaluation)
Show a list of CV syllables (가, 거, 고, 구, 나, 다, 마, 바).
For each:
Prompt: “Read this out loud, then tap to hear the model.”
After tapping, play native audio.
Ask small reflection question:
“Did your initial consonant sound softer, more aspirated, or very tense?”
User chooses one; this can feed into recommendations.weakness for series training.[1]

6. Phase 2 mini‑quiz – small but dense questions
Use your existing quizQuestions setup but follow the global rules.[1]
Suggested structure:
Visual recognition (3 items):
“Which consonant represents the n‑sound at the front of your mouth?”
“Which consonant uses lips and often sounds like b/p?”
Ear recognition (3 items):
Hear CV syllable → choose consonant.
One plain vs aspirated minimal pair item (e.g., “Which has more air: syllable 1 or 2?”).
Special ㅇ (1–2 items):
Show 아 vs 앙 and ask about ㅇ’s role (silent vs ng).
Hear 앙 → choose final consonant.
Syllable building (1–2 items):
Given consonant + vowel, pick the correct block.
Global behaviour for quiz:
Stem text never directly includes the correct glyph or “letter name” as the answer.
After submit:
Play correct/wrong SFX.
Red highlight on learner’s incorrect option, green on the correct option.
Show quizQuestions[idx].explanation under the options.[1]
If incorrect, also show a short “Correct answer:” line, but in UI, not in the stem itself (as your TSX already hints).[1]
